## The Blank Space of Memory: HabitTrack

---

### Prologue: That Unobtrusive Little Tool

I stumbled upon this little WPF tool called **HabitTrack** in a dusty corner of a niche independent developer forum. The title of the post was modest, devoid of any flashy buzzwords, simply stating: "A clean habit tracker."

After downloading and unzipping it, the interface was so pristine it felt almost desolate: a standard modern Windows flat style, a deathly white background, an empty task list on the left, a single-line input box on the right with an "Add Task" button, and a line of gray, unclickable text in the bottom right corner: *v1.0.0 © Absolute Studio*.

At the time, I was trapped in a cloud of anxiety, desperately trying to drag my spinning life back on track, so I casually pinned it to my taskbar. Every night before shutting down the computer, accompanied by the final roar of the chassis fan, I would open it and routinely punch in a few lines.

### Days 1–2: A Illusion of Control

Everything followed a comforting, logical progression.

With utmost discipline, I stuffed the input box with words brimming with hope: "Review advanced mathematics," "Memorize 50 vocabulary words," "Run 3 kilometers around the lake." I clicked add, watched them turn into a list with checkboxes, and then checked them off one by one with my own hand. In that moment, I felt a fleeting illusion that I was fighting back against the quicksand of life. When I cold-booted the computer again the next morning, the records from the previous night lay there obediently, like a loyal, silent scribe.

I even began to depend on this illusion of control.

### Day 3: The Midnight Witness

The shift happened on an otherwise unremarkable morning.

The night before, I had been so utterly exhausted from rushing a project that I collapsed into bed right after taking a shower. I hadn’t even opened the computer, let alone added any tasks. Yet, when I pressed the power button the next morning and clicked on HabitTrack while rubbing my bleary, aching eyes, a checked record had abruptly appeared at the top of the list, rendered in a cold, bold font:

> **Woke up at 3:33 AM**

My hand gripping the mouse froze instantly.

The room was so quiet I could hear nothing but my own breathing. I racked my brain trying to search my memory and vaguely recalled waking up once in the middle of the night, parched with thirst. I remembered pulling back the curtain to look out at the dead, silent street. But that was just a fleeting few seconds of blurred consciousness; I hadn’t even turned on the light, much less touched the computer.

Maybe... I was sleepwalking? Or had I mixed up the dates? I rubbed my temples, forcing down the faint uneasiness in my chest, assuming it was some peculiar bug with the system time or network synchronization.

### Day 5: Seventeen Minutes of Absence

When a similar incident occurred for the second time, excuses began to fail.

That day, right above the tasks I had entered myself, a line of text cut sharply across the list:

> **Stared blankly at the screen for 17 minutes**

The moment I saw those words, a chill shot straight up my spine.

I remembered. It was around eleven o'clock the previous night. I had hit a dead end while writing code, and my entire being had slipped into a state of near-numb emptiness. I hadn’t moved the mouse, hadn't tapped the keyboard; I had just stared blankly at the blinking cursor on the screen, letting my mind go completely vacant. I vividly remembered that when my consciousness finally refocused, I glanced at the time in the bottom right corner—indeed, nearly twenty minutes had passed.

It was watching me. It wasn't tracking the "habits" I chose to type in; it was tracking "me." Its interface remained so clean and restrained—no pop-ups, no errors, and the CPU usage stayed firmly at a modest 0.1%. This extreme normalcy made it feel all the more unnervingly eerie.

### Day 7: The Sycamore Tree After the Rain

On the seventh day, when I opened that white window again, it didn’t display any of today's tasks. The entire interface looked like a sheet of old correspondence, yellowed by water damage. Upon it sat a single line of text, each digit like a blunt knife plunging precisely into the raw flesh of my deepest memories:

> **2019.06.15 — Confessed to her after failing math, rejected**

My heart dropped like a stone, skipping a beat entirely.

That was the summer of my sophomore year in high school. That day, my final mathematics exam paper had been violently punctured by red ink with a glaring score: "48." Meanwhile, she was the top student in the class, her name always hanging high at the very peak of the bulletin board like an unreachable star.

On that particular evening, the air was thick with the humid, subtly sweet scent of damp earth and green grass right after a sudden torrential downpour. I stood beneath a massive sycamore tree by the school gate, my shadow stretched long and distorted by the setting sun. I held a letter written on ordinary grid paper tightly in my hand; its edges had turned white and wrinkled from the excessive force of my grip, the paper's edge biting painfully into my fingertips.

When she walked out of the school building, time seemed to slow down. The fading glow of the setting sun traced the contour of her profile, draped over her like a gentle, pale gold veil. Her hair was long, the ends carrying a natural, slight curl that swayed lightly in the summer breeze. A few stray strands of hair on her forehead were brushed aside by the wind, revealing eyes so clear they held not a speck of dust, resembling a lake filled with sunlight in early summer. She wore nothing but the most ordinary, even slightly oversized school uniform white shirt, yet on her, it looked like a cloud of pristine cloud that defied desecration, making one feel that even approaching her required an immense amount of courage. She walked toward me, her lips habitually curling upward, her eyes sparkling with the fine, fragmented pride and tenderness unique to that age.

I can’t remember how I managed to move my feet to block her path. I only remember that my voice was shaking terribly: "I... I like you."

She froze visibly, a flash of startled confusion crossing her clear eyes. Then, that confusion melted into a gentle smile carrying a touch of apology. She tilted her head slightly and said softly, "You should probably worry about passing math first."

In that fraction of a second, the world seemed to hit a mute button. The deafening chirp of the cicadas vanished instantly, replaced by a distant, hollow ringing in my ears. I tried desperately to force a polite, presentable smile onto my face, nodding mechanically to her, but my throat felt as though it were stuffed with dry sand, making every single breath carry a heavy, dull ache.

The next morning, as I stood by the school gate pushing my bicycle, I saw her from a distance walking side by side with a boy from the neighboring class. The boy was tall, his smile bright and effortless as he naturally carried her backpack, which was printed with a cartoon pattern. She turned her head to listen to him speak, the morning sun catching her long eyelashes and casting a near-dreamlike halo around her.

I stood in the surging crowd of students, my palms slick with cold sweat, watching their figures grow distant. It wasn't a heart-wrenching pain; rather, it was a profound, suffocating sorrow creeping through my bones, laced with deep insecurity. It was the world pushing you away in the gentlest manner possible, then whispering in your ear: *"Look, you aren't good enough, so you don't even have the right to stand by her side."*

### Day 9: The Sanctuary Behind the Screen

On the ninth day, HabitTrack added another recent, equally hidden secret to its logs:

> **2024.11.02 — Watched 4 hours of VTuber livestream in the middle of the night**

Staring at this line, my back went entirely numb.

In this real world where even stepping out to throw away the trash requires putting on a mask, I hid myself incredibly well. I liked watching those virtual streamers, indulging in the colorful illusions woven by pixels and voice actors. Because in that world, separated by a cold screen, they would never look down on my mediocrity or awkwardness, nor would they mock the fact that I was still single and had accomplished nothing. They always laughed with boundless energy, using voices so gentle they could melt ice to interact with every single comment. Even if I was just the most unnoticeable handle among tens of thousands of scrolling messages, I could still pretend, through that sweet "Thank you all for the company," that my drifting, anchorless soul was briefly held.

But this was my most shameful secret. I had never mentioned it to anyone, nor had I ever left a single word about it in any digital diary on my computer.

At 12:30 AM that night, the world had gone to sleep, leaving my room illuminated only by the faint blue glow cast by the monitor. I curled up in my gaming chair, the headphone cable acting like an oxygen tube. Her bright, cloudless laughter flowed into my ears—a voice with a strange magic, neither too high nor too low, always carrying a tiny, dependent pout at the end of each sentence. During those four hours, I fell into a sort of delusion, feeling as though every time she blinked or smiled, she was doing it directly for the solitary me on this side of the screen.

The virtual avatar on the screen wore cat-ear headphones, her massive eyes sparkling like stars. I knew it was nothing more than a carefully orchestrated commercial performance; I knew she couldn't see me and didn't care who I was, yet my finger trembled as I typed into the chat box: *"I had a lot of fun today too, thank you."*

In reality, I couldn't remember how long it had been since I looked a living human in the eye and said the word "happy." In this virtual sanctuary, there were no grades, no KPIs, and no cold judgments from others. She would laugh, read out the names of donations loudly, and say in an energetic tone: "Thank you for always being here!" In that moment, the immense self-deception transformed into honey, and I almost managed to fool myself—that in some corner of the universe, someone truly anticipated my arrival.

Until the stream went black and the feed cut off.

When the words "Stream Ended" popped up, the large monitor darkened instantly, reflecting my pale, unshaven, and profoundly exhausted face. The room was flooded with a deathly silence, leaving only the numb hum of the PC tower's cooling fans and the faint static hiss lingering in the headphones. The sense of emptiness in that moment crashed over me completely. It was a hundred times colder than walking home alone in the freezing rain after work.

### Day 10: I Will Remember For You

On the tenth day, when I habitually pressed the power button, HabitTrack was no longer that mild-mannered WPF window.

The pristine white background had been entirely swallowed up, replaced by a thick, oppressive blackness stretching across the screen. Following that, the task list vanished; there were only rows of pixelated text, bright and crimson like fresh blood, rolling slowly upward through the darkness at an eerie tempo:

> **Failure**
> **Jealousy**
> **Loneliness**

These words acted like a mirror, tearing open the inner core I had tried so desperately to hide and gloss over for all these years, exposing it naked to the open air. Finally, the scrolling red text ground to a halt, and a line of text began to flash continuously in the center of the screen like a silent invitation, or perhaps an unalterable covenant:

`[Press any key to remember her face]`

My fingers hovered above the keyboard, my fingertips icy cold. I knew that the moment I pressed down, those wounds so painstakingly scabbed over by time would be torn open all over again; I knew this software was peeling away the armor I wore to pretend I was strong. Yet, when humans confront their deepest suffering, they often possess a morbid urge to indulge in it. As if punishing myself, I closed my eyes and heavily struck the spacebar.

The screen immediately plunged into a blinding, dead white.

The intense glare stung my eyes so badly I could barely keep them open. A few seconds later, the white light receded, and a line of text written in an incredibly soft, near-sighing white font appeared in the center of the screen:

> **If you forget the pain, you forget yourself.**

I stared blankly at those words.

The air in the room felt frozen. In that fraction of a second, what welled up from the depths of my being wasn't terror, nor was it the urge to run away. It was a... near-shuddering relief of being completely seen through, completely understood. For so many years, everyone had been telling me to "look forward," to "forget the past," and to "be strong." Only this bizarre piece of software spoke to me from the darkness: *Your pain is the very proof of your existence.*

A second later, HabitTrack closed automatically.

The desktop returned to its original state. But right in the middle of that cluttered pile of icons, a brand-new `.txt` file sat quietly. The three characters making up the filename were my actual name.

With a trembling hand, I double-clicked to open it. Inside, there were no complex codes, nor were there any blood-red words. There was only a single line of quiet, black text:

> **I will remember for you.**

A cold breeze drifted in through the crack of the unlatched window, making the curtains rustle softly. I looked at the file, and suddenly felt that in this deserted midnight, I was no longer entirely alone.
